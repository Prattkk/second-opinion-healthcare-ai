# Physician Discovery Interview Guide

## Purpose

Validate the core problem hypothesis: physicians spend significant time reconstructing patient history from fragmented records, and a patient-initiated structured summary would improve point-of-care workflow.

All questions are designed to be non-leading, non-promotional, and HIPAA-conscious. The goal is discovery, not persuasion.

---

## LinkedIn Outreach Template

> Hi Dr. [Last Name],
>
> I'm a graduate student exploring a patient-side tool that converts fragmented medical records — PDFs, lab reports, prescription images — into a structured clinical timeline a patient can hand to any provider.
>
> Before building anything, I'm speaking with practicing physicians to understand how patient records actually show up at the point of care — and what's missing.
>
> Would you be open to a 10–15 minute conversation? There's no agenda other than learning from your experience. No recording, no sales pitch.
>
> If a call doesn't work, I'd also appreciate 2 minutes on a short survey. Thank you for considering it.

---

## Interview Questions (10–15 minutes)

### Opening (2 min)
1. What's your specialty and typical patient volume?
2. How many of your patients are managing conditions across multiple providers?

### Problem Validation (5 min)
3. When a patient arrives with records from another provider, how does that information typically reach you?
4. How often do you feel you're making decisions with incomplete history?
5. Can you describe a recent case where fragmented records affected the visit?

### Solution Reaction (3 min)
6. If a patient walked in with a one-page structured summary of their full medical history — diagnoses, meds, allergies, recent labs — how would that change your workflow?
7. What format would be most useful? (chronological timeline, problem list, medication summary)
8. What would make you trust it — or not trust it?

### Closing (2 min)
9. What's the single biggest friction point in your workflow related to patient records?
10. Is there anything else you'd want a tool like this to do — or explicitly not do?

---

## Quick Survey (Async Alternative)

For physicians who prefer a 2-minute survey over a call:

1. **How often do you see patients with incomplete medical history?** (1–5 scale: Never → Every visit)
2. **If a patient brought a structured one-page summary, would you reference it?** (Yes / Maybe / No / I wouldn't use it)
3. **What would the summary need to include for you to trust it?** (Multiple choice: Source attribution / Verification disclaimer / Medication reconciliation / All of the above)
4. **What format would be most useful?** (Timeline / Problem list / Medication summary / Combination)
5. **What concerns would you have about a patient-generated AI summary?** (Open text)

## Targeting Priority

| Priority | Physician Type | Rationale |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| 1 | Primary Care / Family Medicine | See patients with history at 3–4+ institutions |
| 2 | Chronic Disease Specialists (cardiology, endocrinology, oncology) | Manage long-term multi-provider patients |
| 3 | Internal Medicine Hospitalists | Complex case exposure, high-pressure history reconstruction |
| 4 | ER / Urgent Care Physicians | Maximum urgency for clinical history |
| 5 | Independent Practice Physicians | Less institutional friction, faster adoption signal |

## Signal Interpretation

**Strong demand signals:**
- "I wish patients came in with something like this"
- "I spend the first 5–10 minutes of every visit piecing things together"
- Asks unprompted about how to get it for their patients

**Low demand signals:**
- "Our EHR handles most of this"
- "I usually just ask the patient"

**Red flags:**
- HIPAA liability concerns (address with patient-controlled framing)
- Accuracy distrust (address with verification disclaimer)
- "I'd only use it if it came from the EHR" (institutional dependency — different market)

**Go/No-Go threshold:** 8 of 15 physicians citing the fragmentation pain, 60%+ agreeing the summary format would be useful.
